I thought I was ranking opportunities. Instead, I stumbled into a question I still do not have an answer to.

A few weeks ago I published a framework for finding market gaps. It scored industries on pain, frequency, market size, and competition. The rankings felt right. Then I kept using it, and something kept happening. The same problem would come up in very different conversations. Healthcare came up and everyone saw a business. Immigration came up and everyone agreed it mattered, but founders walked away. The problem had not changed. The person looking at it had.

The score did not know who was reading it. That turned out to matter enormously.

I started comparing markets, industries, and countries side by side. At first I thought I was improving a scoring framework. What I eventually realized was that opportunities do not have a single ranking. They have audiences. And every audience is asking a different question.

The Moment It Got Interesting

Same problem. Same score.
Very different paths.

Healthcare administration is the one I kept coming back to. US hospitals spent $43 billion in 2025 just collecting payments insurers already owed. Score it with any framework and it tops the list. Score the same problem in India and it comes out almost as high. Both correct. But sit with both side by side, and the paths to building something are completely different.

US HEALTHCARE ADMIN
The path to revenue is short.

Hospitals have IT budgets, procurement departments, and dedicated software spend. A founder can build a SaaS tool and have a paying customer within a year.

vs.
INDIA HEALTHCARE ADMIN
The path is a completely different journey.

55% of healthcare spending is out of pocket. 40% of providers cite cost as the main barrier to going digital. The conversion is harder, the resistance is different, the timeline is longer.

Only 53% digitalized · EY India 2025

I started writing down every factor that seemed to matter across these comparisons. Twelve made the list. Eight survived. I call what is left The Builder's Map, because a map looks different depending on where you are standing.

The Builder's Map

Eight factors. Two layers.

Opportunity
Is this worth solving?
Pain · Frequency · Willingness to Pay · Economic Cost · Competition Gap
Execution
Can you actually reach it?
Infrastructure · Regulatory Ease · Cultural Adoption

Scored separately, not blended. An Opportunity of 9 and Execution of 2 is not a 5.5. It is "incredible opportunity, brutal to build." The Execution layer is where countries diverge most.

Three Lenses

Same eight factors.

Each set of weights I tried turned out to be a person.

These three are starting points, not the whole list. A VC reads this map differently than a solo founder. A government planner reads it differently than a nonprofit.

That immigration result kept nagging at me, though. The framework was doing exactly what I built it to do. It took me a while to see why that bothered me.

One thing worth holding onto

The lenses are not types of people. They are types of questions. The same person can pick up one lens for one problem and a different lens for another.

Where I Get Stuck

The rabbit hole I was
not expecting.

I have to be honest. I was feeling pretty good about all of this. The factors made sense. The lenses made sense. The map was holding up across every comparison I threw at it. I was excited about where this was going. Then I came across a podcast that challenged everything I had built.

It was a My First Million episode with Barry Ritholtz, the fund manager. Somewhere in the middle, someone brought up Zillow. The founder, Rich Barton, looked at housing data in the early 2000s. Messy. Unstructured. Locked away with real estate agents. No homeowner had ever paid for organized property information. By every signal that existed at the time, it was not a business.

He built it anyway. And the world adjusted around him.

That made me sit back. If I had run Zillow's moment through my own framework honestly, every score would have said walk away. High pain, sure. But low willingness to pay. No clear buyer. No existing budget. The kind of profile that today looks a lot like immigration looks on my map.

The framework sees the present. It cannot see what is about to shift.

That is what the example unlocked for me. The framework measures data. It cannot measure human behavior. It cannot measure the moment when a population decides to trust software for something they used to do offline. It cannot measure conviction. It cannot measure the years between "nobody pays for this" and "everyone pays for this."

I have stopped wanting to label problems "social impact" just because willingness to pay is low today. That feels too neat. Some of what we treat as social impact right now might be the next decade's biggest business. The behavior has not shifted yet. The buyer is not assembled yet. But behavior shifts. Buyers assemble. Markets emerge where there were none.

So I want to leave you with two questions instead of an answer.

QUESTION 01

Which behavioral shift are you watching that nobody else is pricing in yet?

QUESTION 02

Which of today's "no buyer" problems do you think will quietly become the biggest businesses of the next decade?

If something comes to mind, I would love to hear it. Any pattern hiding in plain sight, any shift you think is about to happen, anything I got wrong.
